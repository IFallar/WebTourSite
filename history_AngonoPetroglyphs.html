Angono Petroglyphs

The Angono Petroglyphs, discovered in 1965, are thought to be the Philippines' oldest known artworks. They are a collection of 127 figural carvings carved on the wall of a shallow cave of volcanic tuff dating back to the third millennium B.C. They were designated a National Cultural Treasure in 1973 and were added to the World Inventory of Rock Art in 1985. The National Museum of the Philippines developed multiple molds of the carvings almost immediately after they were brought to public attention. Following that, the property was cleaned and prepared for conservation in the 1980s. By the early 1990s, the Angono Petroglyphs were under threat from regional development. Following an inquiry, the significance of the site was fully acknowledged, and steps were taken to safeguard the land and the sculptures. Despite being spared utter destruction, there was concern that new road building and blasting into the hill behind the petroglyphs may jeopardize the cave's stability. The altered earth also aroused concerns about the site's increasing vulnerability to water damage. The petroglyphs had eroded over time due to uncontrolled growth and animals.
